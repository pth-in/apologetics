Did Jesus actually exist, or is He just a made-up story? 

You might see people on the internet claiming that Jesus never existed. However, this idea is almost never taken seriously by real professionals. Historians and experts—even those who are not religious at all—agree on one thing: **Jesus was a real person in history.** Famous skeptical scholars like Bart Ehrman strongly agree that there is no reason to doubt that Jesus existed.

Why are experts so sure? Because the evidence is so strong. In this post, we won't be using the Bible or simply relying on faith to prove our point. Instead, we are going to look at outside sources—like Roman governors, Jewish historians, and physical artifacts dug up from the ground—to build the case for Jesus.

---

## 1. Roman Records: The Expert Historians

If we want unbiased evidence, the careful records of the massive Roman Empire are the best place to start. 

### Tacitus
Cornelius Tacitus is known as one of the greatest Roman historians. In his book [*Annals* (Book 15, Chapter 44)](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.02.0078%3Abook%3D15%3Achapter%3D44), written around AD 116, he talks about a massive fire in Rome under Emperor Nero. Nero needed someone to blame, so he blamed a new group called Christians:

> *"Nero fastened the guilt and inflicted the most exquisite tortures on a class hated for their abominations, called Christians by the populace. Christus, from whom the name had its origin, suffered the extreme penalty during the reign of Tiberius at the hands of one of our procurators, Pontius Pilatus..."*

Tacitus did not like Christians at all. Yet, he confirms exactly what the Bible says: the time, the place, and the man responsible (Pontius Pilate) for the death of Christ.

### Suetonius
Another famous Roman historian named Suetonius wrote around AD 112 in his book [*Life of Claudius* (25.4)](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Suetonius/12Caesars/Claudius*.html#25.4). He mentions that the Emperor kicked the Jews out of Rome because they were always fighting over "Chrestus." 

Experts know that "Chrestus" was a common misspelling of "Christus" (Christ). This shows that even decades after Jesus died, Roman leaders were still dealing with the massive impact of Him and His followers.

---

## 2. The Jewish Testimony

### Flavius Josephus
Flavius Josephus was a famous Jewish historian who worked for the Romans in the first century. He was not a Christian. In his massive history book [*Antiquities of the Jews* (Book 20, Chapter 9, Section 1)](http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0146%3Abook%3D20%3Asection%3D197), written around AD 93, Josephus calmly mentions an event involving Jesus' family:

> *"Now there was about this time... the brother of Jesus, who was called Christ, whose name was James, and some others, and when he had formed an accusation against them as breakers of the law, he delivered them to be stoned."*

Historians love this kind of simple mention. Since Josephus just plainly refers to "the brother of Jesus... called Christ" without trying to preach or argue, historians consider it very authentic. To Josephus, the fact that Jesus existed wasn't even up for debate. 

---

## 3. Hostile & Neutral Observers

### Pliny the Younger (AD 112)
Pliny the Younger was a Roman governor dealing with the "problem" of growing Christians. In his [*Letters 10.96*](https://faculty.georgetown.edu/jod/texts/pliny.html), he writes to Emperor Trajan asking what to do with them. In his report, he accidentally documents what early church services looked like:

> *"They were in the habit of meeting on a certain fixed day before it was light, when they sang in alternate verses a hymn to Christ, as to a god..."*

### Mara Bar-Serapion
Sometime after AD 70, a Syrian philosopher named [Mara Bar-Serapion](https://www.earlychristianwritings.com/text/mara.html) wrote a letter from prison to his son (which is kept today in the British Museum). He compares the death of Jesus to the unfair deaths of famous thinkers like Socrates:

> *"What advantage did... the Jews [gain] by murdering their Wise King? ...The wise king did not die for good; he lived on in the teaching which he had given."*

### Lucian of Samosata (The Satirist)
In the second century, a Greek writer named Lucian loved to make fun of Christians in his work [*The Death of Peregrinus*](https://scaife.perseus.org/reader/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0062.tlg042.perseus-eng4:11-13/). Even though he is mocking them, he accidentally proves the crucifixion happened:

> *"The Christians, you know, worship a man to this day... [who] was crucified on that account... they have sinned by denying the Greek gods and by worshipping that crucified sage himself..."*

---

## 4. The Archaeological Proof

Beyond old books and letters, physical items dug up from the dirt also prove the history of the Bible.

- **[The Pilate Stone](https://www.imj.org.il/en/collections/395572-0):** For hundreds of years, skeptics argued that Pontius Pilate was a made-up character because we hadn't found any physical proof of him. That changed in 1961 when scientists dug up a massive stone block with a clear carving: *"Pontius Pilate, Prefect of Judea."* 
- **[The Caiaphas Ossuary](https://cojs.org/ossuary_of_the_high_priest_caiaphas-_18-36_ce/):** In 1990, construction workers in Jerusalem accidentally found an ancient burial cave. Inside was a fancy bone-box (ossuary) for a high priest. The carving read: *"Joseph, son of Caiaphas"*—these are the actual physical remains of the High Priest who ran the trial of Jesus. 

---

## Conclusion

When we put all the pieces together, the timeline of history is very clear. We have angry Roman politicians, unbiased Jewish historians, and huge physical artifacts all proving the facts about the life of Jesus. From a strictly historical viewpoint, there is far more proof that Jesus existed than there is for many other ancient kings and emperors!

History has definitely proven that He lived, taught, and died on a cross by the orders of Pontius Pilate. But a dead teacher is just a small footnote in a history book.

**The biggest question is: If history proves He died, did He stay dead?**
